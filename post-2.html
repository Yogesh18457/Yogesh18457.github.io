India's First Private Orbital Rocket Reaches Space as Vikram-1 Aces Debut Flight

By Yogesh · July 19, 2026

India crossed a new threshold in space technology on July 18, as Skyroot Aerospace's Vikram-1 became the first privately built Indian rocket to reach orbit — making India the third country in the world with demonstrated private orbital launch capability.

The 23-metre rocket lifted off from the Satish Dhawan Space Centre in Sriharikota, following a 35-minute delay after a technical issue was flagged just five minutes before liftoff. Once airborne, Vikram-1's three solid-fuel stages performed as planned, followed by a liquid-propellant kick stage — called the Orbital Adjustment Module — which fired for roughly six minutes to fine-tune the satellite's position. Fifteen minutes after liftoff, the company confirmed the rocket had reached its target orbit of 450 kilometers.

The mission, named Aagaman — Sanskrit for "the arrival" — carried Skyroot's own SCOPE satellite to gather performance data, along with two symbolic payloads: a small 18-karat gold rocket by artist Ajay Kumar Mattewada, and a lab-grown gem piece from Cosmos Diamonds.

Vikram-1 is entirely carbon-composite in construction, a first for an Indian orbital launch vehicle, and builds directly on Skyroot's 2022 Vikram-S suborbital test flight — India's first privately developed rocket to reach space, though that mission stopped short of orbit. Former ISRO chairman S. Somanath has taken on an honorary advisory role with Skyroot ahead of this launch.

Reacting to the mission's success, Lt. Gen. AK Bhatt (retired), Director General of the Indian Space Association, called it a defining milestone for India's space journey.

Skyroot isn't stopping here — the company has a larger vehicle, Vikram-II, in development for 2027, designed to carry payloads up to 900 kilograms using a cryogenic engine, a significant step up from Vikram-1's capacity.

The successful flight arrives at a notable moment for Indian spaceflight more broadly, coming shortly after ISRO — the government space agency — experienced setbacks with its own PSLV rocket, adding extra weight to Vikram-1's success as a sign of where India's growing private space sector is headed.
